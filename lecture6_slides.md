# Lecture 6: Dry Bulk Terminal Design and Material Handling Systems

## Slide 1 - Lecture 6: Dry Bulk Terminal Design and Material Handling Systems
- Port Engineering Course
- From berth interface to stockyard and outloading
- Focus: throughput, layout, and operational reliability
- Speaker cue: Today we move from waterfront structures into cargo-flow engineering.

## Slide 2 - Learning Outcomes
- Map the dry bulk cargo-flow chain from ship to stockyard to gate/plant
- Compare common unloading and conveying systems
- Estimate first-pass throughput and storage requirements
- Identify environmental, safety, and reliability constraints
- Speaker cue: The goal is to see the terminal as one integrated flow system.

## Slide 3 - Why This Lecture Matters
- Dry bulk cargo dominates many Indonesian and industrial ports
- Terminal bottlenecks directly control vessel turnaround time
- Poor stockyard or conveyor design creates hidden OPEX penalties
- Speaker cue: A berth is only productive if the downstream chain can keep up.

## Slide 4 - Core Flow Logic
- Vessel arrives at berth
- Cargo is unloaded into hopper or receiving system
- Material moves by conveyor to stockyard or direct plant feed
- Stacker forms stockpiles; reclaimer returns cargo to outbound system
- Speaker cue: The slowest step defines the whole chain.

## Slide 5 - Main Terminal Components
- Berth pocket and marine interface
- Ship unloader or mobile harbor crane
- Hopper, feeder, and transfer towers
- Conveyors and stockyard equipment
- Truck / rail / plant loading interface
- Speaker cue: Every component must be sized with the same flow logic.

## Slide 6 - Unloader Options
- Grab ship unloader: flexible and common, moderate spillage control challenge
- Continuous ship unloader: cleaner and efficient for finer cargo streams
- Mobile harbor crane: useful for flexible or smaller-volume operations
- Speaker cue: Selection depends on cargo type, variability, and CAPEX strategy.

## Slide 7 - Conveyor System Considerations
- Belt width, speed, and bulk density define capacity
- Transfer points are common dust and spillage hotspots
- Reliability depends on idlers, belt condition, and power redundancy
- Speaker cue: Conveyors are often underestimated because they look simple.

## Slide 8 - Stockyard Functions
- Buffer ship arrivals from inland demand variability
- Blend cargo quality where needed
- Provide surge capacity for weather or maintenance interruptions
- Speaker cue: Storage is not idle land; it is operational insurance.

## Slide 9 - Stockpile Arrangement Concepts
- Longitudinal stockpiles for linear flow
- Chevron or windrow stacking for blending
- Keep traffic, fire access, and drainage paths clear
- Speaker cue: Layout affects safety, reclaim efficiency, and contamination control.

## Slide 10 - Reclaim and Outloading
- Reclaim by bucket wheel, portal reclaimer, loaders, or direct truck loader
- Match reclaim rate to plant demand or shiploading target
- Avoid mismatch between storage and dispatch equipment
- Speaker cue: Reclaim can become the bottleneck even if unloading is fast.

## Slide 11 - Bottleneck Logic
- System capacity = minimum reliable capacity of the chain
- Example sequence: unloader -> feeder -> conveyor -> stacker -> reclaimer
- One undersized transfer point can limit the whole terminal
- Typical bottleneck causes: truck delay, warehouse not ready, weather disruption, equipment failure, admin delay, and cargo mismatch
- Speaker cue: Optimize the chain, not just one machine.

## Slide 12 - Preliminary Sizing Equations
- Required average throughput = annual demand / effective operating hours
- Nominal design rate = required average throughput / utilization factor
- Storage tonnage = daily demand x target storage days
- Yard area depends on bulk density, stacking height, and access allowance
- Speaker cue: These are screening tools before detailed design.

## Slide 13 - Worked Example: Inputs
- Annual demand = 4.0 million t/year
- Effective operating time = 5,500 h/year
- Utilization target = 0.70
- Storage autonomy = 10 days
- Bulk density = 1.6 t/m3
- Speaker cue: This is a realistic concept-level planning case.

## Slide 14 - Worked Example: Results
- Required average throughput is about 727 t/h
- Nominal design chain rate is about 1,040 t/h
- Daily demand is about 10,960 t/day
- Ten-day storage is about 109,600 t
- Practical yard footprint will exceed the pure volume calculation once lanes and berms are included
- Speaker cue: Layout factors often double the minimum theoretical area.

## Slide 15 - Environmental and Safety Controls
- Dust suppression: water spray, covers, enclosed transfer points, wind fences
- Stormwater and runoff management
- Fire and self-heating risk for coal or biomass
- Contamination and housekeeping control
- Speaker cue: Environmental control is part of design, not an add-on.

## Slide 16 - Reliability and Redundancy
- Provide maintenance access and spare paths where justified
- Design transfer towers and conveyors for inspection and safe shutdown
- Consider N+1 logic for critical feeders or belts in high-value operations
- Speaker cue: Availability can matter as much as nameplate capacity.

## Slide 17 - Key Performance Indicators
- Berth occupancy ratio (BOR)
- Net unloading rate (t/h)
- Conveyor availability (%)
- Stockyard utilization (%)
- Vessel turnaround time and demurrage exposure
- Speaker cue: These KPIs link engineering decisions to business outcomes.

## Slide 18 - Case Discussion
- Site: multipurpose industrial port with coal and clinker imports
- Constraint: strong winds, limited backland, and seasonal rain
- Task: propose equipment and stockyard strategy
- Speaker cue: Your answer should balance flexibility, dust control, and throughput.

## Slide 19 - Lifecycle Perspective
- Low CAPEX can create high maintenance and demurrage cost later
- Dust complaints and spillage often become recurring operating risks
- Expansion paths should be reserved early in the layout
- Speaker cue: Plan for future scale-up before the land is locked in.

## Slide 20 - Lecture Summary
- Dry bulk terminal design is a cargo-flow problem, not just a berth problem
- The bottleneck controls practical throughput
- Stockyard, environmental control, and reliability shape long-term performance
- Next step: extend the same logic to specialized terminal systems and productivity analysis
- Speaker cue: A good bulk terminal is safe, clean, and consistently reliable.

## Backup Slide A - Quick Equipment Comparison

### Grab Unloader
- Flexible
- Common and proven
- More spillage/dust management needed

### Continuous Unloader
- Cleaner flow
- Good for steady bulk streams
- More specialized and capital-intensive

### Mobile Harbor Crane
- Highly flexible
- Good for mixed-use berths
- Lower dedicated productivity in large-scale bulk service
