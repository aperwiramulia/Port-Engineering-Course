# Lecture 5: Quay Walls and Berthing Structures

## Slide 1 - Lecture 5: Quay Walls and Berthing Structures
- Port Engineering Course
- Structural systems, berthing loads, and design checks
- Focus: concept-to-preliminary design decisions
- Speaker cue: Today we bridge geotechnical conditions, vessel operations, and structural reliability.

## Slide 2 - Learning Outcomes
- Distinguish gravity, sheet pile, and pile-supported systems
- Build a first-pass berth load model
- Estimate berthing energy and select preliminary fender class
- Identify governing ULS and SLS checks
- Speaker cue: You should be able to justify a quay concept by the end of this session.

## Slide 3 - Why This Lecture Matters
- Berths are critical operational bottlenecks
- Structural errors cause downtime and safety risks
- Good concept selection reduces lifecycle cost
- Speaker cue: A quay that is safe but operationally inefficient is still a poor design.

## Slide 4 - Quay Structure Families
- Gravity walls
- Sheet pile walls
- Pile-supported wharves
- Each responds differently to soil, depth, and loading
- Speaker cue: These are not interchangeable systems.

## Slide 5 - Gravity Wall: How It Works
- Resists lateral actions mainly by self-weight
- Typical forms: caisson, mass concrete, block structures
- Key checks: sliding, overturning, bearing capacity
- Best with competent foundation soils
- Speaker cue: Gravity solutions are robust, but foundation quality is decisive.

## Slide 6 - Sheet Pile Wall: How It Works
- Embedded wall with flexural resistance
- Often uses anchors, tie-rods, and deadman systems
- Key checks: wall bending, deflection, anchor force
- Typical advantage: faster construction
- Speaker cue: Speed and footprint can be strong advantages.

## Slide 7 - Pile-Supported Wharf: How It Works
- Deck on vertical or raked piles
- Open structure, less retained-soil action
- Key checks: pile axial and lateral capacity, connection detailing
- Preferred for weak compressible soils and seismic concern
- Speaker cue: Often the most practical solution for difficult ground.

## Slide 8 - Concept Selection Logic
- Strong soil + constrained footprint -> gravity wall
- Moderate retained heights + schedule pressure -> sheet pile wall
- Soft soils or high seismicity -> pile-supported wharf
- Validate with geotechnical and lifecycle assessment
- Speaker cue: Select by evidence, not habit.

## Slide 9 - Load Categories for Berthing Structures
- Permanent loads: self-weight, pavement, utilities
- Operational loads: cargo, trucks, crane wheel loads
- Earth and hydrostatic pressures
- Berthing and mooring actions
- Environmental loads: wave, current, wind, seismic
- Speaker cue: Missing one load family can invalidate the concept.

## Slide 10 - Berthing Energy Basics
- Concept equation: E = 1/2 M V^2 x coefficients
- Inputs: vessel mass, approach velocity, correction factors
- Output: design berthing energy demand
- Must pair with structural reaction check
- Speaker cue: Energy alone is not enough.

## Slide 11 - Fender Function and Selection
- Absorb impact energy
- Control reaction transferred to structure
- Satisfy stroke and deflection compatibility
- Ensure acceptable hull pressure with panel system
- Speaker cue: A higher-capacity fender can still be wrong if reaction is too high.

## Slide 12 - Mooring Actions and Consequences
- Drivers: wind, current, wave drift
- Effects: bollard loads, line tension, deck and local stress
- Poor mooring layout affects operations and safety
- Speaker cue: Serviceability issues often start with mooring demand.

## Slide 13 - ULS Checks (Strength and Stability)
- Sliding, overturning, bearing (gravity systems)
- Bending, shear, anchor capacity (sheet pile systems)
- Pile axial, lateral, and group effects (pile wharf)
- Local checks at fender and bollard zones
- Speaker cue: ULS protects against instability and failure.

## Slide 14 - SLS Checks (Functionality)
- Wall movement and rotation limits
- Deck deflection and rail alignment
- Crack width and durability exposure limits
- Fender displacement compatibility
- Speaker cue: SLS governs operability and maintenance performance.

## Slide 15 - Worked Example: Inputs
- Design vessel mass: M = 6.0e7 kg
- Approach velocity: V = 0.15 m/s
- Coefficients: CM = 1.10, CE = 0.90, others = 1.00
- Compute concept-level berthing energy
- Speaker cue: This is a preliminary estimate framework.

## Slide 16 - Worked Example: Result
- E = 0.5 x 6.0e7 x (0.15)^2 x 1.10 x 0.90
- E is approximately 6.68e5 J (approximately 668 kJ)
- With design margin, required class is around 1000 kJ
- Next: verify associated fender reaction against structure
- Speaker cue: Passing energy check without reaction check is a common mistake.

## Slide 17 - Frequent Failure Modes
- Underestimated berthing velocity
- Ignored eccentric impact effects
- Weak detailing at anchorage zones
- Underdesigned marine durability and corrosion allowance
- Incomplete geotechnical representation
- Speaker cue: Most failures trace back to assumptions and detailing.

## Slide 18 - Case Discussion (Class Activity)
- Site: soft clay to 20 m, berth depth -14 m
- Conditions: moderate-high seismicity, heavy STS operation
- Task: choose structural concept and justify checks
- Speaker cue: Defend your choice using load path and soil behavior.

## Slide 19 - Lifecycle Design Perspective
- Compare CAPEX + maintenance + downtime + adaptability
- Consider construction risk and schedule sensitivity
- Plan inspection and replacement accessibility early
- Speaker cue: Lowest CAPEX is not lowest total project cost.

## Slide 20 - Lecture Summary
- Quay type selection = soil + operation + risk
- Berthing design = energy and reaction compatibility
- ULS + SLS + durability must be integrated
- Next step: detailed components and foundation interaction
- Speaker cue: The best berth concepts are structurally sound and operationally resilient.

## Backup Slide A - Quick Comparison

### Gravity Wall
- Strong soils preferred
- High stiffness
- High material volume

### Sheet Pile Wall
- Fast installation
- Deflection, anchor, and corrosion sensitive
- Good for moderate retained heights

### Pile-Supported Wharf
- Good for weak soils and seismicity
- Higher detailing complexity
- Strong operational adaptability
