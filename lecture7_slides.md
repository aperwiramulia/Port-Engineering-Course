# Lecture 7: Sedimentation and Dredging

## Slide 1 - Lecture 7: Sedimentation and Dredging
- Port Engineering Course
- Why ports shoal and how dredging keeps navigation reliable
- Focus: sediment source, transport, hot spots, dredging methods, and selection criteria
- Speaker cue: This lecture links coastal processes directly to maintenance cost and navigation reliability.

## Slide 2 - Learning Outcomes
- Identify the main sediment sources affecting port channels and basins
- Explain the main transport mechanisms that move sediment into port waters
- Recognize the common hot spots of sedimentation inside a port system
- Compare dredging methods and justify selection criteria
- Speaker cue: Students should leave with both morphology understanding and practical dredging logic.

## Slide 3 - Why This Lecture Matters
- Ports are designed to create calm water, but calm water also encourages deposition
- Shoaling reduces safe draft, increases delay risk, and raises recurring OPEX
- Dredging is not only a construction activity; it is often a long-term maintenance strategy
- Speaker cue: Sedimentation is a port-performance issue, not just a coastal-process issue.

## Slide 4 - Main Sediment Sources
- River discharge and catchment erosion
- Alongshore sediment supply from littoral drift
- Resuspension of existing seabed material by waves, currents, or propeller wash
- Shoreline and channel-bank erosion
- Port-generated sediment from reclamation runoff or disturbed surfaces
- Speaker cue: The source diagnosis matters because control options differ for each source.

## Slide 5 - Mechanism 1: Bed Load Transport
- Coarser grains move by rolling, sliding, or saltation near the bed
- Driven by current and wave-induced shear stress
- Important for sandy entrances and exposed channels
- Speaker cue: Bed load is strongly linked to bottom shear and local bedform movement.

## Slide 6 - Mechanism 2: Suspended Load Transport
- Fine sediment stays in the water column due to turbulence
- Particles settle when flow energy drops below the settling threshold
- Common in estuaries, river mouths, and sheltered basins
- Speaker cue: Suspended load explains why fine silt collects in low-energy harbor pockets.

## Slide 7 - Mechanism 3: Littoral Drift and Estuarine Trapping
- Littoral drift moves sand alongshore under oblique wave approach
- Breakwaters interrupt this transport and can create entrance shoaling
- Tidal asymmetry and salinity circulation can trap fine sediment inside estuaries
- Speaker cue: Entrance shoaling and inner-estuary siltation are often driven by different physics.

## Slide 8 - Hot Spots of Sedimentation
- Port entrance and breakwater heads
- Breakwater shadow zones
- Turning basins
- Berth pockets and overdeepened areas
- River bends, confluences, and delta-front channels
- Suggested visual: seaport hotspot diagram showing entrance shoaling, sheltered deposition zones, turning basin, berth pockets, and river-mouth accumulation
- Suggested visual alternative: reference multipurpose-port layout with numbered hot spots at the entrance, lee zones, turning basin, quay pockets, and side basins
- Speaker cue: The hotspot map usually follows where water velocity reduces or circulation traps sediment.

## Slide 9 - Why Berth Pockets and Turning Basins Shoal
- They are intentionally wider and calmer than the approach channel
- Vessel maneuvering may resuspend fines, but weak background flow lets them settle again
- Overdepth pockets become local sediment traps
- Speaker cue: Operational geometry can unintentionally create maintenance hot spots.

## Slide 10 - Maintenance vs Capital Dredging
- Maintenance dredging restores the existing design depth after shoaling
- Capital dredging creates, deepens, or widens a channel or basin
- Maintenance dredging is recurring OPEX; capital dredging is project CAPEX
- Speaker cue: The economic framing is different even if some equipment looks similar.

## Slide 11 - Mechanical Dredging Methods
- Backhoe dredger: accurate, strong near structures, suitable for stiffer soils
- Grab or clamshell dredger: flexible for localized work and restricted basins
- Often paired with barges for spoil transport
- Speaker cue: Mechanical dredging is often selected when accuracy and confined working space matter.

## Slide 12 - Hydraulic Dredging Methods
- Trailing suction hopper dredger (TSHD): efficient for loose sand or silt in larger open-water campaigns
- Cutter suction dredger (CSD): continuous pumping, useful for compact soils and reclamation support
- Strongly influenced by spoil transport route and pipeline logistics
- Speaker cue: Hydraulic methods are attractive when production rate and transport efficiency dominate.

## Slide 13 - Typical Dredging Project Steps
- Bathymetric survey and sediment investigation
- Define target depth, side slope, and tolerance
- Check sediment quality and disposal or beneficial-use route
- Select dredger, support fleet, and work window
- Execute with traffic and environmental control
- Verify with post-dredge survey
- Speaker cue: A dredging project is a survey-control-execution-verification cycle.

## Slide 14 - Selection Criteria for Dredging Method
- Soil type and strength
- Required production volume
- Positional accuracy near existing structures
- Distance to disposal or reclamation area
- Wave, current, and navigation constraints
- Environmental permit limits and turbidity tolerance
- Mobilization, schedule, and total cost
- Speaker cue: There is rarely one universal best dredger; selection is site-specific.

## Slide 15 - Quick Screening Logic
- Loose, large-volume channel shoaling often favors TSHD
- Confined berth-pocket work near quay walls often favors backhoe or grab dredging
- Continuous fill pumping may favor CSD
- Contaminated sediment may shift the decision toward methods with tighter containment and disposal control
- Speaker cue: The right answer usually comes from matching soil, geometry, and spoil route together.

## Slide 16 - Risks and Controls
- Turbidity and plume spread
- Interference with navigation traffic
- Overdredging or failure to achieve tolerance
- Disposal-area capacity and environmental compliance
- Weather downtime and equipment availability
- Speaker cue: Dredging success is measured by safe, compliant depth delivery, not just excavation volume.

## Slide 17 - Lecture Summary
- Sedimentation starts with source, transport, and local trapping conditions
- Ports create calm-water zones that often become deposition zones
- Dredging methods differ in accuracy, production rate, and spoil-transport logic
- Method selection should balance soil, geometry, environment, traffic, schedule, and cost
- Speaker cue: Good port engineering reduces sediment problems where possible and manages unavoidable dredging rationally.

## Backup Slide A - Fast Comparison Table

### Backhoe / Grab
- Better near structures
- Good control in confined areas
- Usually lower large-scale production efficiency

### TSHD
- Strong for loose material and routine channel maintenance
- Self-transports spoil
- Less suitable for tight structural work or very hard material

### CSD
- High continuous production
- Good for pumping to reclamation
- Needs pipeline corridor and work-zone control
