## Slide 1 - Lecture 9: Port Operation and Maintenance
- Port Engineering Course
- Keeping ports safe, reliable, and available after construction
- Focus: operation, maintenance strategy, asset criticality, reliability KPIs, and intervention planning
- Speaker cue: This lecture moves from design and asset type into how ports actually stay functional year after year.

## Slide 2 - Learning Outcomes
- Explain why port O&M matters to safety, service, and life-cycle cost
- Distinguish corrective, preventive, predictive, and reliability-centered maintenance
- Identify O&M needs for structures, equipment, utilities, and navigation systems
- Use simple KPIs and risk logic to prioritize intervention
- Speaker cue: Students should connect maintenance decisions to engineering performance, not treat them as housekeeping only.

## Slide 3 - Why This Lecture Matters
- A well-designed port can still fail operationally if maintenance is late or undisciplined
- Downtime at a berth, conveyor, pump, substation, or access gate can stop cargo flow
- Maintenance quality strongly affects availability, safety, and long-term cost
- Speaker cue: Ports are operational systems, not just construction products.

## Slide 4 - Operation, Maintenance, and Engineering Feedback
- Operations observe delays, alarms, unsafe conditions, and abnormal behavior first
- Inspection and monitoring turn symptoms into condition information
- Maintenance planning decides what to fix, when, and with what priority
- Engineering review converts repeated defects into redesign or replacement decisions
- Suggested visual: files/figures/lecture9_om_cycle.svg
- Speaker cue: A strong port organization closes this loop continuously.

## Slide 5 - Main Port Asset Groups
- Marine structures: quay, jetty, dolphins, fenders, bollards, revetment, pavement edge
- Cargo systems: cranes, conveyors, hoppers, pumps, pipelines, valves, loading arms
- Yard and storage systems: pavement, drainage, lighting, stockyard, tank farm, warehouse interfaces
- Utilities and support systems: power, fire water, ICT, standby systems, gate systems
- Navigation and access: channel, basin, aids to navigation, roads, access control
- Suggested visual: files/figures/lecture9_asset_matrix.svg

## Slide 6 - Typical Failure and Degradation Modes
- Corrosion, concrete cracking, spalling, settlement, scour, coating breakdown
- Mechanical wear, misalignment, overheating, hydraulic leakage, sensor fault
- Drainage blockage, pavement rutting, dust-control failure, lighting outage
- Siltation, buoy or beacon failure, utility instability, standby-system weakness
- Speaker cue: Different assets fail differently, so one maintenance strategy never fits all.

## Slide 7 - Maintenance Strategy Types
- Corrective: repair after failure
- Preventive: planned interval-based maintenance
- Predictive or condition-based: intervene from measured degradation trend
- Reliability-centered: choose strategy from failure mode and consequence
- Speaker cue: Good practice is usually a combination, not a single doctrine.

## Slide 8 - Corrective vs Preventive vs Predictive
- Corrective can be acceptable for low-criticality items with minor consequence
- Preventive works well for routine inspections, lubrication, coating, and scheduled replacement
- Predictive is useful where condition data exists: vibration, thermal trend, oil analysis, electrical monitoring
- Reliability-centered maintenance is strongest where consequences differ sharply across components
- Speaker cue: Students should compare strategy by consequence, detectability, and cost.

## Slide 9 - Asset Criticality and Risk
- Criticality depends on safety, environmental, and operational consequence
- Likelihood matters, but consequence often governs priority in ports
- Detectability changes urgency: hidden failure modes need stronger control
- Simple screening: priority ~ consequence x likelihood x detectability
- Speaker cue: Not all visible damage deserves equal urgency.

## Slide 10 - Reliability KPIs
- Availability: time asset is ready for service
- Utilization: time available asset is actually used
- MTBF: mean time between failures
- MTTR: mean time to repair
- Backlog age and condition index help reveal deferred-risk buildup
- Speaker cue: Activity counts alone are weak KPIs if they do not explain service impact.

## Slide 11 - Inspection Planning Logic
- Start with an asset register and criticality ranking
- Define routine visual checks and periodic detailed inspections
- Add monitoring for assets where degradation can be trended
- Link findings to intervention thresholds, not only report writing
- Speaker cue: Inspection without action rules becomes paperwork.

## Slide 12 - Marine Structure O&M Focus
- Splash-zone corrosion and concrete distress
- Fender damage, chain wear, loose anchor bolts, bollard deterioration
- Deck cracking, joint distress, scour, and settlement indicators
- Need for periodic underwater or hard-to-access inspection
- Speaker cue: Berth reliability often depends on small interface components, not only the main deck.

## Slide 13 - Equipment and Utility O&M Focus
- Conveyor misalignment, bearing wear, spillage, and dust-control weakness
- Pump, valve, hose, or loading-arm reliability in liquid bulk service
- Power supply continuity, switchgear condition, standby generator readiness
- Fire protection availability and alarm-system integrity
- Speaker cue: Utility failure can stop a terminal even when the berth itself is structurally sound.

## Slide 14 - Landside and Access O&M Focus
- Yard pavement distress and heavy-vehicle damage
- Drainage blockage and flooding risk
- Lighting, signage, and gate-system performance
- Road congestion as an operational-maintenance issue, not only a traffic issue
- Speaker cue: Landside deterioration often shows up first as reduced productivity and safety margin.

## Slide 15 - Planning Maintenance Windows
- Use low-demand periods, weather windows, and vessel schedule gaps
- Separate urgent corrective work from planned shutdown work
- Coordinate spares, permits, contractor access, and operational isolation
- Avoid creating secondary risk during maintenance itself
- Speaker cue: Maintenance planning is partly engineering and partly logistics.

## Slide 16 - When Repair Is Not Enough
- Repeated fender-anchor damage may indicate wrong berth geometry or poor load path
- Recurrent pavement rutting may indicate drainage or axle-load mismatch
- Chronic conveyor stoppage may reflect transfer-point design weakness or lack of redundancy
- Repeated dredging need may indicate a layout or hydrodynamic trap
- Speaker cue: Repetition is evidence; it should trigger redesign thinking.

## Slide 17 - O&M and Life-Cycle Cost
- Cheap delayed maintenance can become expensive shutdown, emergency, or replacement cost
- Spare strategy and repairability influence true asset cost
- Reliability improvements can reduce queue cost and lost ship-hours
- O&M data should feed future CAPEX decisions
- Speaker cue: The cheapest annual maintenance budget is often not the cheapest life-cycle strategy.

## Slide 18 - Practical Screening Workflow
- Build asset register
- Rank criticality
- Identify failure modes and detectable symptoms
- Select inspection and maintenance strategy
- Track KPIs and backlog
- Review repeated defects for redesign or replacement
- Speaker cue: This is a workable framework even before advanced asset-management software exists.

## Slide 19 - Lecture Summary
- Port performance depends on operation, maintenance, and engineering feedback together
- Different assets need different maintenance logic
- Reliability KPIs help connect technical condition to service performance
- Risk-based prioritization is essential where maintenance resources are limited
- Speaker cue: O&M is part of engineering management, not an afterthought.

## Backup Slide A - Quick Comparison

### Corrective
- Low planning effort
- High risk for critical assets

### Preventive
- Stable and simple to schedule
- Can become inefficient if intervals are too rigid

### Predictive / Condition-Based
- Better timing when good data exists
- Requires monitoring discipline and interpretation
