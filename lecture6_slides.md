# Lecture 6: Dry and Liquid Bulk Terminal Design and Material Handling Systems

## Slide 1 - Lecture 6: Dry and Liquid Bulk Terminal Design and Material Handling Systems
- Port Engineering Course
- From berth interface to stockyard or tank farm and outbound delivery
- Focus: throughput, layout, operational reliability, and safety
- Speaker cue: Today we move from waterfront structures into cargo-flow engineering for both solids and liquids.

## Slide 2 - Learning Outcomes
- Map the cargo-flow chain for both dry bulk and liquid bulk terminals
- Compare common equipment systems for solids and liquids
- Estimate first-pass throughput and storage requirements
- Identify safety, environmental, and reliability constraints in each terminal type
- Speaker cue: The goal is to see each terminal as one integrated flow system with different handling physics.

## Slide 3 - Why This Lecture Matters
- Dry bulk and liquid bulk both dominate industrial-port revenue streams
- Terminal bottlenecks directly control vessel turnaround time and inventory security
- Poor stockyard, pipeline, or tank-farm design creates hidden OPEX and safety penalties
- Speaker cue: A berth is only productive if the downstream transfer and storage system can keep up safely.

## Slide 4 - Two Core Flow Logics
- Dry bulk: vessel -> hopper -> feeder -> conveyor -> stockyard -> reclaim -> outbound system
- Liquid bulk: vessel -> loading arm / hose -> manifold -> pipeline -> tank farm -> pump -> outbound pipeline or truck rack
- Both systems depend on continuous flow and compatible downstream capacity
- Speaker cue: The slowest safe and reliable step defines the whole chain.

## Slide 5 - Dry Bulk Main Components
- Berth pocket and marine interface
- Ship unloader or mobile harbor crane
- Hopper, feeder, and transfer towers
- Conveyors and stockyard equipment
- Truck / rail / plant loading interface
- Speaker cue: Every dry bulk component must be sized with the same flow logic.

## Slide 5A - Why Feeders and Transfer Towers Matter
- Feeders meter cargo discharge from the hopper onto the belt
- Poor feeder control causes surging, belt overloading, and spillage below the skirt zone
- Transfer towers are common dust hotspots because material drops, impacts, and changes direction there
- Good practice: enclosed chutes, skirt sealing, wear liners, dust extraction/suppression, and safe maintenance access
- Speaker cue: In many bulk terminals, cleanliness and reliability problems start at these transfer points.

## Slide 6 - Unloader Options
- Grab ship unloader: flexible and common, moderate spillage control challenge
- Continuous ship unloader: cleaner and efficient for finer cargo streams
- Mobile harbor crane: useful for flexible or smaller-volume operations
- Speaker cue: Selection depends on cargo type, variability, and CAPEX strategy.

## Slide 7 - Conveyor System Considerations
- Belt width, speed, and bulk density define capacity
- Transfer points are common dust and spillage hotspots
- Reliability depends on idlers, belt condition, and power redundancy
- Speaker cue: Conveyors are often underestimated because they look simple.

## Slide 8 - Stockyard Functions
- Buffer ship arrivals from inland demand variability
- Blend cargo quality where needed
- Provide surge capacity for weather or maintenance interruptions
- Speaker cue: Storage is not idle land; it is operational insurance.

## Slide 9 - Stockpile Arrangement Concepts
- Longitudinal stockpiles for linear flow
- Chevron or windrow stacking for blending
- Keep traffic, fire access, and drainage paths clear
- Speaker cue: Layout affects safety, reclaim efficiency, and contamination control.

## Slide 10 - Reclaim and Outloading
- Reclaim by bucket wheel, portal reclaimer, loaders, or direct truck loader
- Match reclaim rate to plant demand or shiploading target
- Avoid mismatch between storage and dispatch equipment
- Speaker cue: Reclaim can become the bottleneck even if unloading is fast.

## Slide 11 - Dry Bulk Bottleneck Logic
- System capacity = minimum reliable capacity of the chain
- Example sequence: unloader -> feeder -> conveyor -> stacker -> reclaimer
- One undersized transfer point can limit the whole terminal
- Typical bottleneck causes: truck delay, warehouse not ready, weather disruption, equipment failure, admin delay, and cargo mismatch
- Speaker cue: Optimize the chain, not just one machine.

## Slide 12 - Dry Bulk Preliminary Sizing Equations
- Required average throughput = annual demand / effective operating hours
- Nominal design rate = required average throughput / utilization factor
- Storage tonnage = daily demand x target storage days
- Yard area depends on bulk density, stacking height, and access allowance
- Speaker cue: These are screening tools before detailed design.

## Slide 13 - Dry Bulk Worked Example: Inputs
- Annual demand = 4.0 million t/year
- Effective operating time = 5,500 h/year
- Utilization target = 0.70
- Storage autonomy = 10 days
- Bulk density = 1.6 t/m3
- Speaker cue: This is a realistic concept-level planning case.

## Slide 14 - Dry Bulk Worked Example: Results
- Required average throughput is about 727 t/h
- Nominal design chain rate is about 1,040 t/h
- Daily demand is about 10,960 t/day
- Ten-day storage is about 109,600 t
- Practical yard footprint will exceed the pure volume calculation once lanes and berms are included
- Speaker cue: Layout factors often double the minimum theoretical area.

## Slide 15 - Liquid Bulk Main Components
- Berth or jetty with marine loading arms or certified transfer hoses
- Ship-shore manifold, custody-transfer metering, and emergency shut-down system
- Transfer pumps, pipelines, valves, pigging or drain arrangement where relevant
- Tank farm with bunds, firefighting system, and truck / pipeline dispatch interface
- Suggested visual: liquid bulk flow chain from vessel manifold to tank farm
- Speaker cue: Liquid bulk design replaces stockyard logic with hydraulic transfer and containment logic.

## Slide 16 - Liquid Bulk Flow and Storage Logic
- Cargo moves by pressure head and pump energy rather than by belts and reclaimers
- Tanks provide buffer storage, batch segregation, settling time, and emergency containment margin
- Separate lines or strict line-clearing procedures may be required for incompatible products
- Suggested visual: tank-farm layout with bunding, pump house, and truck rack
- Speaker cue: In liquid bulk, contamination, leakage, and overpressure can be more critical than visible spillage.

## Slide 17 - Liquid Bulk Preliminary Sizing Equations
- Required average transfer rate = annual demand / effective operating hours
- Nominal pump and line design rate = required average rate / utilization factor
- Tank storage volume = daily demand x storage days / product density
- Pipeline diameter depends on allowable velocity, pressure loss, viscosity, and pump duty
- Speaker cue: Liquid bulk screening must check both inventory volume and hydraulic transfer limits.

## Slide 18 - Liquid Bulk Worked Example
- Annual demand = 3.2 million t/year of fuel product
- Effective operating time = 6,000 h/year
- Utilization target = 0.75
- Product density = 0.85 t/m3
- Storage autonomy = 12 days
- Result: required average transfer rate about 533 t/h, nominal design rate about 710 t/h, daily demand about 8,770 t/day, and storage need about 123,800 m3
- Speaker cue: Even moderate liquid demand can require a large tank farm once storage autonomy is included.

## Slide 19 - Safety and Environmental Controls
- Dry bulk: dust suppression, runoff control, fire access, spillage cleanup, and wind management
- Liquid bulk: bund walls, overfill protection, leak detection, foam system, hazardous-area zoning, and emergency shut-down logic
- For both: maintenance access, isolation philosophy, training, and realistic operating procedures are part of design
- Speaker cue: Safety systems are not optional accessories; they are core terminal infrastructure.

## Slide 20 - Comparative Design Checks
- Dry bulk asks: can the receiving, conveying, and stockyard chain sustain the vessel program?
- Liquid bulk asks: can the arms, pumps, pipelines, and tanks sustain transfer safely within product constraints?
- Common design checks: berth occupancy, transfer rate, storage autonomy, reliability, expandability, and consequence of failure
- Speaker cue: The engineering tools differ, but the systems-thinking mindset is the same.

## Slide 21 - Case Discussion
- Dry bulk case: coal and clinker import terminal with limited backland, strong wind, and rain exposure
- Liquid bulk case: fuel import terminal with shared jetty, fire-safety setback requirements, and truck-loading peaks
- Task: propose one key bottleneck and one key layout reserve for each case
- Speaker cue: Students should compare how the same port faces different risks for solids versus liquids.

## Slide 22 - Lecture Summary
- Bulk terminal design is a cargo-flow problem, not just a berth problem
- Dry bulk depends on receiving, conveying, stockyard, and reclaim logic
- Liquid bulk depends on transfer arms, pipelines, pumps, tanks, and containment logic
- Safety, environmental control, and reliability shape long-term performance in both systems
- Speaker cue: A good bulk terminal is productive, safe, and operationally resilient.

## Backup Slide A - Quick Equipment Comparison

### Grab Unloader
- Flexible
- Common and proven
- More spillage/dust management needed

### Continuous Unloader
- Cleaner flow
- Good for steady bulk streams
- More specialized and capital-intensive

### Mobile Harbor Crane
- Highly flexible
- Good for mixed-use berths
- Lower dedicated productivity in large-scale bulk service

### Marine Loading Arm + Pump System
- Controlled closed transfer for liquid cargo
- Supports metering, ESD logic, and product-specific handling
- Needs disciplined maintenance, seal integrity, and fire-safe design
